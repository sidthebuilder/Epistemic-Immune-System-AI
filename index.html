Abstract

Enterprise knowledge infrastructure suffers from a class of failures that retrieval-centric architectures cannot address: epistemic degradation. Decisions become stale without being superseded. Hypotheses accumulate citations until they are indistinguishable from evidence. Unresolved questions decay below retrieval thresholds, rendering institutional ignorance invisible. Existing epistemic memory architectures leave three structural gaps unresolved: (1) decay rates remain insensitive to domain-specific temporal dynamics; (2) no continuous monitoring layer detects degradation between classification events; and (3) no mechanism exists for resolving cross-departmental epistemic contradictions without violating data sovereignty.

This paper introduces three tightly integrated contributions. Domain Velocity Epistemic Decay (DVED) corrects the misspecification of class-uniform decay by introducing a learnable domain velocity coefficient v_d ∈ [0,1] that modulates the effective forgetting rate as a function of how rapidly the relevant external world changes. The Epistemic Immune System (EIS) provides a continuous monitoring layer that maintains a five-dimensional health vector H(KO) = [h_status, h_staleness, h_ignorance, h_adversarial, h_darkmatter] per Knowledge Object, with formal detection signatures for five categories of epistemic pathogen—including the novel category of Epistemic Dark Matter (structural voids inferred from topological anomalies in the knowledge graph). Zero-Knowledge Epistemic Cascades (ZKEC) resolve cross-departmental epistemic contradictions using zk-SNARK proofs at the graph-edge level, propagating importance-score decay across isolated departmental ledgers without exposing underlying proprietary content.

To our knowledge, this is the first framework to (a) formally specify domain-velocity correction for epistemic decay, (b) provide a continuous multi-dimensional epistemic health monitor with adversarial detection, (c) introduce topological void detection as an epistemic pathogen category, and (d) apply zero-knowledge cryptography to cross-organizational epistemic conflict resolution.

Section 1

Introduction

1.1 The Retrieval-Fidelity Ceiling

Enterprise AI research has converged on retrieval fidelity as the primary lever for improving system reliability. The progression from dense retrieval to hybrid search to GraphRAG represents genuine progress within a well-defined optimization surface: given a query, retrieve the most semantically relevant content as efficiently as possible. Yet this surface has a ceiling that no retrieval improvement can breach.

Retrieval systems operate on an epistemically flat substrate. A semantically optimal retrieval pipeline returns the most relevant content—but relevance is computed over semantic distance, not epistemic authority. The system may retrieve a binding board decision, an abandoned brainstorming memo, a contested claim under active revision, and an unresolved inquiry. Each document is processed with equal epistemic weight. The LLM synthesizes a fluent, coherent, and fundamentally misleading response. Kumar (2026a) terms this the coherence trap.

This is not a retrieval failure. It is an epistemic infrastructure failure. No embedding model, reranker, or retrieval strategy can distinguish an authoritative Decision from an abandoned Hypothesis without access to metadata that the retrieval layer does not model.

1.2 The Three Structural Gaps

Recent work has established a foundation for epistemic memory in organizational AI. OIDA (Bottino et al., 2026) introduces typed Knowledge Objects (KOs) with class labels—Decision, Evidence, Hypothesis, Question, Observation, Plan, Constraint, Narrative, Evaluation—and class-specific decay functions. Kumar (2026a) extends this with the Federated Epistemic Ledger (FEL) and Workflow-Based Epistemic Decay. Despite this progress, three structural gaps remain.

Gap 1: Decay Misspecification. OIDA’s decay function assigns the same forgetting rate to all KOs of class C regardless of domain. A Decision about a product roadmap (domain half-life: days) and a Decision about a regulatory requirement (domain half-life: years) receive identical treatment. Karhade (2026) demonstrates that uniform decay—even class-specific uniform decay—performs 18× worse than no temporal weighting at all in mixed-domain retrieval tasks.

Gap 2: No Continuous Health Monitoring. Between workflow events, the epistemic state of the knowledge base is unmonitored. Status creep, staleness, ignorance collapse, and adversarial manipulation all accumulate silently and go undetected (Gerlach & Lange, 2026; CIO.com, 2026; Kaesberg et al., 2026).

Gap 3: No Cross-Departmental Conflict Resolution. The FEL architecture federates epistemic authority but provides no mechanism for detecting contradictions across ledger boundaries without breaching data sovereignty. When Engineering records a Decision that contradicts a confidential Legal constraint, the conflict is invisible by construction.

1.3 Contributions

  1. Domain Velocity Epistemic Decay (DVED): A formal correction to OIDA-class decay introducing a learnable domain velocity coefficient modulating the effective forgetting rate, estimated from organizational supersession data via survival analysis.
  2. The Epistemic Immune System (EIS): A continuous monitoring architecture with a five-dimensional health vector per KO, formal detection signatures for five pathogen categories including the novel Epistemic Dark Matter category, tiered alert-response protocol, and immune memory.
  3. Zero-Knowledge Epistemic Cascades (ZKEC): A novel mechanism applying zk-SNARK proofs at graph-edge level to detect cross-departmental epistemic contradictions and propagate importance-score cascades without revealing proprietary content.
  4. The Epistemic Context Graph (ECG): Integration of the above into a formally specified extension of the Gartner context graph standard.
Section 2

Background and Related Work

2.1 Epistemic Memory Architectures

Bottino et al. (2026) introduce OIDA, the first formal epistemic memory layer for organizational AI. KOs carry class-specific exponential decay functions, signed contradiction edges, dependency and supersession relations, and provenance metadata. The OIDA architecture explicitly separates epistemic classification from semantic retrieval. Kumar (2026a) extends OIDA to enterprise scale with the Federated Epistemic Ledger and Workflow-Based Epistemic Decay, and formalizes adversarial hardening requirements.

2.2 Adaptive Temporal Decay

Karhade (2026) addresses the temporal misspecification problem directly, introducing a continuous decay surface parameterized by velocity (frequency of observation) and volatility (magnitude of change between observations, measured via embedding distance). The critical empirical finding—18× performance degradation from uniform decay—motivates the domain velocity correction introduced in this paper. Weaviate (2026) provides a complementary practitioner framing, introducing “context rot” and domain-specific half-life parameters.

2.3 Silent Organizational Degradation

Multiple independent research threads document silent epistemic degradation: the AI Knowledge Trap (Gerlach & Lange, 2026), Agentic Drift (CIO.com, 2026), Problem Drift in multi-agent systems (Kaesberg et al., 2026), the Augmentation Trap (Levin, 2026), and Semantic Reward Collapse (2026). No existing work proposes a unified monitoring architecture detecting these degradation modes at the knowledge-object level.

2.4 Context Graphs and Decision Traces

Gartner (2026) positions context graphs as foundational infrastructure for agentic AI, predicting adoption in over 50% of agentic systems by 2028. Context graphs extend knowledge graphs with decision traces capturing process (“how” and “why”) in addition to content. The critical limitation, as identified by InfoWorld (2026) and Klarity.ai (2026), is the absence of epistemic status: a decision trace records that a decision occurred but not whether it is still binding, provisional, superseded, or hypothetical.

2.5 Digital Immunology

The epistemic immune system metaphor originates in AI safety practitioner discourse (Cybernative.AI, 2025; LessWrong, 2026). Existing proposals are metaphorical and non-formal; no prior work provides a formal architecture with detection signatures, health metrics, and response protocols. This paper provides the first such formalization.

Section 3

Domain Velocity Epistemic Decay (DVED)

3.1 The Misspecification Problem

The OIDA decay function for a Knowledge Object KO with class C is:

I(KO, t) = I₀ · δ(C, t) where δ(C, t) = exp(−λ_C · t)

This assigns the same class-level rate λ_C to all KOs of class C regardless of domain. The assumption is empirically violated across all organizational domains.

3.2 The DVED Correction

We introduce a domain velocity coefficient v_d ∈ [0,1] for each domain d. The DVED-corrected effective decay function is:

δ_effective(C, d, t) = δ(C, t) · (1 − α · v_d)

where α ∈ (0,1] is a global scaling parameter bounding the maximum velocity correction. The corrected importance score is:

I_DVED(KO, t) = I₀ · δ_effective(C, d, t) + ∑ injection_signals − ∑ contradiction_penalties

The standard OIDA decay is recovered when v_d = 0 for all domains. Fast-moving domains (high v_d) accelerate decay; slow-moving domains (low v_d) suppress it.

3.3 Velocity Estimation

Domain velocity is the empirical supersession rate over a rolling time window T:

v_d = |{supersession events in domain d over T}| / (|{active KOs in domain d}| · T)

A supersession event requires dist(emb(v_new), emb(v_old)) > θ_supersession—distinguishing genuine change from mere re-observation. Velocity parameters are initialized from domain taxonomy priors and continuously updated from organizational data via survival analysis (Kaplan-Meier per domain).

Section 4

The Epistemic Immune System (EIS)

4.1 Architecture Overview

The EIS is a continuous monitoring service operating over the Epistemic Context Graph. It maintains, for each KO, a health vector:

H(KO) = [h_status, h_staleness, h_ignorance, h_adversarial, h_darkmatter] ∈ [0,1]&sup5;

where 0 denotes a healthy epistemic state and 1 denotes critical degradation. The aggregate health score is:

H_agg(KO) = max(h_status, h_staleness, h_ignorance, h_adversarial, h_darkmatter)

The EIS scanner runs on a configurable interval (recommended: 4 hours) and updates all health vectors continuously.

4.2 Pathogen Taxonomy

Category 1: Status Creep

Definition. A KO of class C is cited as if it carries the epistemic authority of a higher class C′ (where Hypothesis < Observation < Evidence < Decision).

Detection Signature. For a Hypothesis H, define the citation authority ratio:

R(H) = |{citations of H as Evidence or Decision}| / |{total citations of H}|

Status Creep is flagged when R(H) > τ_status over a trailing 30-day window. Recommended initialization: τ_status = 0.30.

Health Component. h_status = min(1.0, R / τ_status)

Category 2: Staleness

Definition. A high-authority KO (class Decision or Evidence) has received no reinforcement signal within its domain-velocity-adjusted refresh interval.

Detection Signature.

t_last(D) = elapsed time since last reinforcement signal θ_D(d) = θ_baseline · (1 + (1 − v_d)) [θ_baseline = 90 days]

Staleness is flagged when t_last(D) > θ_D(d).

Health Component. h_staleness = min(1.0, t_last / θ_D(d))

Category 3: Ignorance Collapse

Definition. An unresolved Question Q has decayed below the retrieval importance threshold I_min, rendering institutional ignorance invisible to agents and operators.

Detection Signature. Flagged when I_DVED(Q) < I_min and t_open(Q) > θ_Q (recommended: θ_Q = 30 days). The condition identifies Questions that are simultaneously old, unanswered, and invisible.

Health Component. h_ignorance = max(0, 1 − I_DVED(Q) / I_min) when I_DVED < I_min, else 0.

Category 4: Adversarial Anomalies

Definition. A statistically anomalous reclassification of KOs at the department or corpus level, indicative of insider manipulation, agent hallucination cascades, or automated poisoning attacks.

Detection Signature. Let V_trans(t) be the number of KOs reclassified Hypothesis→Decision within a 24-hour window. Flagged when:

V_trans > μ_trans + 3σ_trans

Additionally, any Hypothesis→Decision transition without a cryptographically signed human governance event is flagged unconditionally, regardless of velocity.

Health Component. h_adversarial = 1.0 (binary quarantine) when either condition is triggered.

Category 5: Epistemic Dark Matter (Structural Voids)

Definition. A region of the knowledge graph where KOs are absent but topologically expected given surrounding density and centrality. Structural Voids represent unknown unknowns: the organization does not know what it does not know, and no Question KO has been created to surface the gap.

Detection Signature. For each node n in the ECG, compute eigenvector centrality c(n). A Structural Void is detected at n when:

c(n) > τ_centrality AND |{outgoing validation edges from n}| < τ_validation

Response. The EIS automatically synthesizes a Question KO with importance_score = c(n)—converting an unknown unknown into a known unknown, making the gap visible to agents and operators.

Health Component. h_darkmatter = min(1.0, c(n) / τ_centrality) when void condition is triggered.

4.4 Immune Memory

The EIS maintains an epistemic memory core: a persistent store of confirmed pathogen signatures. When a detected pattern is confirmed harmful by governance review, its signature is stored. Future occurrences matching the stored signature are detected with reduced latency and elevated initial confidence—implementing an adaptive immune response analogous to antibody memory in biological systems.

Section 5

Zero-Knowledge Epistemic Cascades (ZKEC)

5.1 The Cross-Departmental Sovereignty Problem

The Federated Epistemic Ledger (Kumar, 2026a) distributes epistemic authority across organizational units, each maintaining a private local ledger. This creates a structural detection gap: when Engineering records a Decision that contradicts a confidential Legal constraint, the contradiction is invisible to both departments. Neither can expose its ledger content to perform cross-validation without violating sovereignty. This is a fundamental tension between two legitimate requirements: epistemic completeness and data sovereignty.

5.2 The ZKEC Mechanism

Zero-Knowledge Epistemic Cascades resolve this tension using zk-SNARK (Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-Interactive Argument of Knowledge) proofs at the graph-edge level.

Step 1 — Proof Generation

When a department records a new Decision KO, it computes a commitment to its local epistemic ledger state:

commitment_d = Commit(G_d, r)

where G_d is a Merkle root over the department’s Knowledge Object graph and r is a random blinding factor. It then generates a zk-SNARK proof π attesting to a predicate over G_d without revealing G_d itself.

Step 2 — Contradiction Detection

The Epistemic Router maintains a registry of inter-departmental constraints as predicates over committed graph states. On new commitment submission:

verify(π, commitment_d, predicate_ij) → {CONSISTENT, CONTRADICTED}

If CONTRADICTED, the Router knows an epistemic conflict exists between departments i and j—without knowing the content of either graph.

Step 3 — Cascade Propagation

On contradiction detection, importance scores cascade across all KOs downstream of the implicated Decision:

I_DVED(KO_downstream) ← I_DVED(KO_downstream) · λ_cascade

The Engineering project manager receives an alert: “Cross-departmental epistemic void detected. Downstream tasks affected. Human review required.” No Legal content is exposed.

5.3 The Privacy Guarantee

The ZKEC mechanism provides the following formal guarantee: the Epistemic Router learns only that a contradiction exists and which downstream nodes are affected. It learns nothing about the content of either department’s private ledger. This is guaranteed by the zero-knowledge property of the underlying proof system (Groth16 or PLONK protocol): a valid proof attests to the truth of the predicate while revealing no additional information.

Section 6

The Epistemic Context Graph (ECG)

6.1 Extension of the Context Graph Standard

Gartner (2026) defines the context graph as connecting “data, states, actions, and goals into a single graph used for agentic AI.” We extend the context graph standard with three epistemic dimensions at every decision trace node n:

  1. Epistemic Class EC(n): OIDA-style classification — Decision, Plan, Evidence, Hypothesis, Observation, Question, Constraint, Narrative, Evaluation
  2. Domain-Velocity Modulated Importance Score IDVED(n): Updated continuously by the DVED layer based on time elapsed, domain velocity, injection signals, and contradiction penalties.
  3. Health Vector H(n): Five-dimensional health assessment updated on each EIS scanner cycle.

6.2 Query-Time Enrichment

When an AI agent queries the ECG, the response includes: retrieved KO content; EC(n) with provenance (classifier, timestamp, workflow signal); IDVED(n) with trajectory annotation (rising / falling / stable); H(n) with active alert flags; and a human-readable Epistemic Summary such as: “This Decision was classified 47 days ago in a Standard-velocity domain. It has received 1 reinforcement signal in the past 30 days. Current health: Yellow (h_staleness = 0.52).”

Section 7

Reference Implementation

We specify a reference implementation as five interoperating microservices:

  • Service 1: Velocity Estimator. Python service computing domain velocity v_d daily from the ECG’s supersession event log via Kaplan-Meier survival analysis. Writes to velocity registry. Target: daily batch completion under 10 minutes.
  • Service 2: DVED Modulator. Middleware intercepting all importance score update events, applying δ_effective(C, d, t) = δ(C, t) · (1 − α · v_d). Configurable α. Target latency: sub-10ms per KO.
  • Service 3: EIS Scanner. Scheduled service (default: 4-hour interval) scanning all KOs for five pathogen conditions. Maintains in-memory immune memory store. Target: full scan of 10&sup6; KOs under 60 seconds.
  • Service 4: ZKEC Router. Accepts commitment submissions from departmental ledger agents. Maintains constraint predicate registry. Evaluates proofs via zk-SNARK verifier (Groth16 or PLONK). Propagates importance-score cascades on contradiction detection. Target: proof verification latency under 500ms.
  • Service 5: ECG Enrichment API. REST endpoint returning enriched nodes with EC, IDVED, and H fields. Target: sub-100ms per query at 10K QPS.

Technology Stack (Reference): Python 3.11 (FastAPI), PostgreSQL 16, Redis 7, snarkjs / circom (zk-SNARK), Docker Compose (local), Kubernetes (production).

Section 8

Evaluation Methodology

We propose a four-stage evaluation program:

  1. Stage 1: DVED Offline Validation. Compare Epistemic Coherence Score (ECS) across: (a) OIDA with class-uniform decay, (b) OIDA with DVED, (c) oracle with ground-truth importance scores. Hypothesis: DVED reduces the performance gap between conditions (a) and (c) by at least 40% on mixed-velocity domain retrieval tasks.
  2. Stage 2: Synthetic Pathogen Injection. Against a synthetic ECG with known ground-truth health states, inject all five pathogen types at controlled rates. Target EIS precision/recall: Status Creep ≥ 0.70/0.80, Staleness ≥ 0.85/0.90, Ignorance Collapse ≥ 0.80/0.90, Adversarial ≥ 0.95/0.95.
  3. Stage 3: ZKEC Precision Evaluation. Using paired departmental ledgers with injected contradictions at known rates, measure contradiction detection precision/recall, false positive rate for cascade propagation, proof generation/verification latency distribution, and data leakage under adversarial proof-crafting.
  4. Stage 4: Organizational A/B Deployment. In a partner organization, deploy ECG + EIS + ZKEC alongside a control condition over 90 days. Measure decision reversal rate, time-to-contradiction-detection, agent hallucination rate on epistemic boundary queries, and analyst trust calibration scores.
Section 9

Conclusion

Domain Velocity Epistemic Decay corrects the fundamental misspecification in class-uniform decay by introducing domain-specific velocity coefficients learnable from organizational supersession data. This directly addresses the 18× performance degradation identified by Karhade (2026).

The Epistemic Immune System provides the first formal continuous monitoring architecture for organizational knowledge health, defining five pathogen categories with precise detection signatures. The novel Epistemic Dark Matter category extends prior work by detecting structural absence of knowledge that topological analysis suggests should exist.

Zero-Knowledge Epistemic Cascades resolve the fundamental tension between epistemic completeness and data sovereignty. By applying zk-SNARK proofs at the graph-edge level, ZKEC enables detection of cross-departmental epistemic contradictions and propagation of importance-score cascades without exposing proprietary content.

Open Research Questions

  1. DVED calibration: What is the appropriate functional form for the velocity correction? Does it generalize across organizational types?
  2. EIS threshold calibration: At what false-positive rates do EIS alerts lose practitioner trust?
  3. ZKEC overhead: Which zk-SNARK protocol (Groth16, PLONK, STARKs) provides the best latency-security tradeoff for enterprise graph sizes (10&sup6;–10&sup9; edges)?
  4. Dark Matter validation: Do EIS-detected Structural Voids correlate with genuine organizational knowledge gaps confirmed by domain experts?
  5. Cross-organizational extension: Can ZKEC extend to inter-organizational knowledge sharing (suppliers, regulators, partners)?
